Protection Policies against deepfake

  • Protection policies against deepfakes aim to safeguard individuals and society from the risks posed by AI-generated manipulated content, such as fake images, audio, and videos.
  • In the UK, recent legislation has taken significant steps to address these threats, particularly focusing on the creation and sharing of non-consensual intimate images.
  • The new laws, including amendments to the Online Safety Act 2023 (click here for more details), and the introduction of the Data (Use and Access) Bill (click here for more details), make it a criminal offence to create or distribute sexually explicit deepfakes without consent.
  • These measures empower law enforcement to prosecute offenders and require platforms to swiftly remove harmful content, with enforcement action from Ofcom (click here for more details) for non-compliance.
  • The UK’s approach reflects a growing commitment to protecting victims, deterring malicious use, and ensuring accountability in the digital age.

In the UK, deepfake protection policies in 2025 are focused primarily on criminalizing the creation and sharing of sexually explicit deepfakes and protecting victims from non-consensual intimate image abuse. Key aspects of the UK’s approach include:


Criminal Offences:

  • Creating or sharing sexually explicit deepfake images without consent is now a specific criminal offence, punishable by up to two years in prison and unlimited fines.
  • New laws also criminalize the taking of intimate images without consent and the installation of equipment with intent to commit such acts.

Platform Responsibility:

  • Social media and online platforms are required to promptly remove deepfake content upon notification and face enforcement action from Ofcom if they fail to comply.
  • Sharing or threatening to share intimate images, including deepfakes, without consent is already an offence under the Sexual Offences Act 2003, as amended by the Online Safety Act 2023.

Victim Support:

  • The new legislation prioritizes protection for victims, particularly women and girls, by closing legal loopholes and expanding the definition of offences to cover both creation and dissemination of deepfakes.
  • The government has committed to further measures, including new offences for requesting the creation of deepfake images and additional provisions in the Crime and Policing Bill.

Gaps and Ongoing developments:

  • While the focus is on intimate image abuse, there is currently a patchwork of existing laws covering other deepfake harms (e.g., defamation, fraud, intellectual property), with broader regulation still under development.
  • The Data (Use and Access) Act 2025, not yet fully in force, will further criminalize the creation and requesting of intimate deepfakes without consent.
